Three-Sided Coin: Bayesian binomial test

Description:

This data set, "Three-Sided Coin", provides the number of times that each of two experimental coins landed on its edge (out of 1,000 tosses).

These data come from a YouTube video on a StandupMaths channel (https://youtu.be/-qqPKKOU-yY) made by Matthew Parker.

Imagine a coin so thick that it has a probability of a third to land on its edge. Matthew Parker made two thick coins (one thicker than the other), threw them 1,000 times and recorded how many times the two coins landed on their edge. 

This example JASP file demonstrates the use of Bayesian binomial test using summary statistics. We will conduct two Bayes factor tests to assess whether any of the two coins is close to being a "three-sided coin" (a coin that has 1/3 probability of landing on its edge). 

For this analysis, we do not need the raw data, but the summary statistics will suffice:

  • The 'thin' coin landed 132 times on its edge out of 1,000 tosses
  • The 'thick' coin landed on its edge 410 times out of 1,000 tosses

If either of those coins was a true three-sided coin with probability of 1/3 of landing on its edge, we would expect about 333 edge outcomes from the 1000 trials. What is the evidence that either of these coins is a true three-sided coin?

S.S. Bayesian Binomial Test

Analysis of the thin coin suggests that the data are much more likely under the alternative hypothesis that the coin is not a proper three sided coin (log(BF10 )= 103):

Bayesian Binomial Test
Successes Failures Test value Log(BF₁₀) p
132 868 0.333 103.141 < .001
Note.  Proportions tested against value: 0.3333.

Prior and Posterior

Under the alternative hypothesis H1 the unknown parameter theta has been assigned a uniform prior from 0 to 1, as indicated by the dotted line. For the thin coin, the resulting posterior distribution for theta is relatively far away from the point that is specified under the "three-sided coin" hypothesis H0: theta = .3333.

S.S. Bayesian Binomial Test

Analysis of the thick coin suggests that the data are more likely under the alternative hypothesis that the coin is not a proper three sided coin (log(BF10 )= 9.56)

Bayesian Binomial Test
Successes Failures Test value Log(BF₁₀) p
410 590 0.333 9.563 < .001
Note.  Proportions tested against value: 0.3333.

Prior and Posterior

In conclusion, the thin coin appears to be too thin, and the thick coin appears to be too thick.
