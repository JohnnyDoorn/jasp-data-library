Results

Description:

This data set, "JASP Webpage Visits", contains the number of visits to the JASP website from January to October 2020.

Variables:

  • date: The date on which metrics were measured in the format YYYY-MM-DD.
  • visits: The number of visits to the JASP webpage (jasp-stats.org).
  • actions: The number of actions executed on the JASP webpage.
  • release: A logical variable that indicates on which date a new version of JASP was released (=1).

This example JASP file illustrates the use of a simple Prophet model (Letham & Taylor, 2018) to predict time series data.

References:

Taylor, S. J. & Letham, B. (2018). Forecasting at scale. The American Statistician, 72(1), 37-45.

This data set was collected by the JASP team.

Prophet

Before fitting Prophet to the data set, it is best to visualize and inspect the history first. Just a brief glimpse at the data reveals a global increasing trend in the number of visits to the JASP web page. There are also two points in the time series where the trend seems to change its direction. These so-called "changepoints" will not be a problem for Prophet because the model can take them into account.

The summary of the posterior distributions shows a positive growth rate, indicating an increasing trend in the number of visits before the first changepoint. The 95% credible interval is narrow and far away from zero, which means that the trend estimate is almost certainly positive (see also the posterior distribution plots below). The MCMC diagnostic statistics (R-hat, ESS [bulk], ESS [tail]) suggest that all chains converged and enough MCMC samples were drawn to precisely estimate the parameter statistics.

Two changepoints were automatically fitted to the data. They roughly coincide with the start and end of the summer break in the academic year. During the summer break, JASP is usually less frequently used by universities and, thus, less people visit the website. This intuition is supported by the first changepoint leading to a negative growth rate (0.581 - 0.978 = -0.397) and a decreasing trend. The second changepoint coincides with a start of a semester and leads to a sharp positive change in the growth rate and increasing trend.

The simulated historical forecast (Letham & Taylor, 2018) displays the mean absolute percentage error for a horizon of 14 days. The error roughtly increases with predictions further within the horizon. This means that predictions are less accurate for days that are further in the future.

Forecast Plots

The overall forecast plot contains the prediction for the data set as well as for 14 future days. The wave-like form of the prediction curve is due to the weekly seasonality included in the model. The predictions seem to fit the data quite well, except for two intervals in early April and September. The future predictions can be expected to have an error range between 10% and 20% of the true values as indicated by the simulated historical forecasts.

Seasonality Plots

The model includes a weekly seasonality effect (7 days) which is displayed here. At the beginning of the week the number of visits seems to increase up to roughly 30% of the average trend. Towards the end of the week the visits decrease down to roughly -40% of the average. This can be explained by decreased activity on weekends.
