Bayesian Sampling Workflow

Procedure

The objective of this sampling procedure is to show with 95% confidence that the percentage of misstatement in the population is lower than the performance materiality of 5%.



The quantity of interest is the misstatement θ in the population. Misstatement is defined as the difference between an item's booked (recorded) value and its audit (true) value. When testing the population misstatement against a given performance materiality, two statistical hypotheses about θ are formulated:



The (null) hypothesis of intolerable misstatement H₊: θ ≥ 5%,

The (alternative) hypothesis of tolerable misstatement H₋: θ < 5%.



The audit risk α is the risk of incorrectly rejecting the hypothesis of intolerable misstatement H₊: θ ≥ 5%. To reject this hypothesis on the basis of a sample, the information from the sample must be sufficient to reduce α to an appropriately low level (i.e., α < 5%).



In a Bayesian analysis, the parameter θ is first assigned a prior probability distribution that incorporates the available audit information about the misstatement in the population. A description and figure of the current prior distribution can be found under the Report section. You can incorporate existing information using the options under the Prior section.

Audit Risk Model

The Audit Risk Model is a method to reduce the required information from the sample on the basis of earlier assessments of inherent risk and control risk, while maintaining the desired audit risk.



Prior to the sampling procedure, the inherent risk was determined to be high (100%). The internal control risk was determined to be medium (52%). According to the Audit Risk Model, the required detection risk to maintain an audit risk of 5% should be 9.62%.

Audit risk (5%) = Inherent risk (high = 100%) x Control risk (medium = 52%) x Detection risk (9.62%)

The translation of the categories High, Medium and Low to probabilities is done using the default settings shown in the table below. To learn more about these default settings and how to adjust them, see the help file of this analysis or look under the Audit Risk Model section.

Planning

The purpose of the planning stage is to find a minimum sample size so that, given a certain number of expected misstatements, the sample provides sufficient information to achieve the specified sampling objectives.



The most likely expected error in the sample is expected to be 2.5%. The minimum sample size that is required for a performance materiality of 5%, assuming the sample contains 4.1 errors, is 164. This sample size is based on the gamma distribution, the a priori assessments of inherent risk ( high ) and control risk ( medium ) from the Audit Risk Model, and the given expected errors.



Consequently, if the intended sample is evaluated and the sum of (proportional) misstatements in the audited items is lower than (or equal to) 4.1, the sample provides sufficient information to conclude that the misstatement in the population is below the performance materiality.

Selection

The purpose of the selection stage is to statistically select a number of sampling units from the population. Sampling units can be individual items (rows) or individual monetary units. The sampling units are selected from the population according to the selection method. To learn more about the current selection method, look under the Method section.



From the population of 3500 non-randomized items, 164 sampling units (monetary units) are selected from the book values ( bookValue ) using the fixed interval monetary unit sampling method.

Evaluation

The purpose of the evaluation stage is to infer the misstatement θ in the population on the basis of a sample.



The population consisted of 3500 items and 1403221 units. The sample consisted of 164 sampling units, of which a total of 5 were misstated. The information from this sample combined with the information in the prior distribution results in a most likely misstatement in the population of 0.02 and a 95% upper bound of 0.044.

Conclusion

The objective of this audit sampling procedure was to determine with 95% confidence whether the misstatement in the population is lower than the specified performance materiality, in this case 5%. For the current data, the 95% upper bound for the misstatement is below the performance materiality.



The conclusion on the basis of these results is that the misstatement in the population is lower than the performance materiality.
